Metal doesn't have one color. It reflects everything around it: streaks of light, distorted reflections, color shifts as the angle changes. Capturing that in a browser used to be a design compromise. Now, with a well-tuned WebGL shader, you can get genuinely convincing metallic surfaces.

## What it does

The component renders a animated metallic surface using a fragment shader. Light streaks travel across the surface, and the color shifts between hues as the angle of simulated light changes over time, like an oil slick or brushed chrome.

## How it works

A GLSL fragment shader runs on a full-canvas quad. The shader uses layered noise functions (simplex or fbm) to generate surface irregularities, then applies a specular highlight calculation based on a simulated light position that moves with time.

Color is determined by mapping surface normals to a hue rotation, creating the iridescent shift. The whole calculation runs per-pixel on the GPU.

## Key properties

- **colorA** - Base color at one angle extreme
- **colorB** - Color at the opposing angle
- **metalness** - How sharp and reflective the highlights appear (default: 0.8)
- **speed** - Speed of light movement across the surface (default: 0.5)
- **roughness** - Surface noise intensity (default: 0.3)
- **iridescence** - Amount of color shift with viewing angle (default: 0.6)

## When to use it

Use this for hero sections, product feature backgrounds, or anywhere you want a premium material feel. It pairs well with dark color schemes where the metallic sheen really pops.

Increase roughness for a brushed metal look. Decrease it for a mirror finish that feels almost liquid.
